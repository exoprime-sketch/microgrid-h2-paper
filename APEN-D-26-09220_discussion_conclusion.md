## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Principal findings

The analysis yields a single, design-actionable picture rather than the qualitative claim that hydrogen becomes attractive when diesel is expensive. The economic crossover between the PV–diesel–battery and PV–battery–hydrogen portfolios is a continuous frontier whose central value is 377 USD MWh$^{-1}$ of delivered diesel at baseline hydrogen cost and a 150 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ carbon price, falling to 342 USD MWh$^{-1}$ once the resilience value of avoided unserved energy is internalised, and ranging from 216 to 423 USD MWh$^{-1}$ as hydrogen capital varies over 0.30–1.20× (§3.2). Crucially, the position of that frontier is governed overwhelmingly by PV capital cost—2.5 times more strongly than by hydrogen-tank cost and two orders of magnitude more strongly than by electrolyser or fuel-cell efficiency (§3.3)—because the hydrogen portfolio is intrinsically PV-intensive (14 MW versus 2.8 MW) to balance a 33.5%-efficient round-trip. The resilience advantage of the hydrogen portfolio is not an artefact of its larger backbone: on an identical PV–battery backbone the diesel–battery portfolio sustains roughly one hour of a worst-case 48-h outage while the hydrogen portfolio sustains the full duration, so the advantage is attributable to the long-duration store itself (§3.4). That advantage is, however, conditional on the diesel-unavailability assumption; relaxing it shows the diesel portfolio matches hydrogen only above clear support thresholds (≥50% derate or ≥12 h fuel) and that any start-up delay—even six hours—collapses its worst-case survivability to zero (§3.5). Finally, the diesel portfolio is dynamically weakest at precisely the diesel-trip contingency where it is energetically weakest, with a 500 ms RoCoF of 5.9 Hz s$^{-1}$ and a 45.9 Hz nadir requiring load shedding, whereas the hydrogen portfolio has no equivalent single-point contingency (§3.6).

### 4.2 Why the result is not self-evident

That hydrogen wins when diesel is dear is, taken alone, unsurprising. The contribution here is the structure behind that statement, which is neither obvious a priori nor recoverable from a coarse break-even calculation. First, the crossover is not set by the hydrogen chain's own cost or efficiency, as intuition might suggest, but by PV capital cost—a consequence of the portfolio's energy balance that only a full 8760-h optimisation reveals, and one that redirects cost-reduction effort away from electrolysers and fuel cells toward PV. Second, the resilience gap survives a matched backbone: a reader could reasonably attribute hydrogen's outage performance to its larger PV and battery, and the matched-backbone experiment is required to show that it does not. Third, the diesel portfolio's resilience is far more fragile than a single-seed or perfect-availability analysis implies; the finding that a six-hour diesel-start delay erases worst-case survivability entirely, while a 50% derate or a 12-h fuel limit does not, is a non-monotone, operationally specific result. Fourth, the most dangerous frequency event for the diesel portfolio is the loss of the very asset that provides its inertia, so that the technology improving small-signal RoCoF also creates the binding large-disturbance contingency. Each of these is a design-actionable, non-obvious refinement of the headline, and together they convert a qualitative intuition into quantitative engineering guidance.

### 4.3 Relation to prior work

The individual techniques used here are established; the contribution is their integration and the attribution it enables. Prior island studies optimise component sizing with tools such as HOMER and mixed-integer programs \cite{P2,P5}, and recent work has tested fuel-constrained or graded-availability outages \cite{P7} and combined techno-economic with dynamic assessment \cite{P9,P10}. What has been missing is a single framework that (i) resolves the cost–resilience trade-off as a continuous, carbon-priced threshold rather than a point estimate, (ii) attributes that threshold to specific design parameters, (iii) isolates the contribution of the flexibility technology from that of the shared backbone, and (iv) verifies dynamic feasibility for the same portfolios and contingencies. The matched-backbone experiment (§3.4) is the methodological centrepiece: by holding the PV–battery spine fixed it separates the effect of the long-duration store from the effect of capacity, which sizing-only optimisations cannot do because they let every component float. Relative to survivability-focused resilience studies \cite{P6}, the present work links the survivability metric to the economic threshold and to a primary-frequency screen on the same system; relative to integrated techno-economic–dynamic studies \cite{P9,P10}, it adds the attribution and technology-isolation analyses and grounds the comparison in a real measured-weather year for a representative Philippine off-grid context \cite{P1,P2}. The Philippine setting is not incidental: with several hundred NPC-SPUG diesel islands and operational PV–battery–diesel microgrids such as Sabang \cite{P1,P2}, delivered diesel costs and outage exposure of the magnitude examined here are realistic rather than hypothetical.

### 4.4 Limitations and future work

Several limitations bound the claims and define the next steps. The dispatch uses a single measured weather year (2025) at one grid cell; interannual variability and, in particular, a typhoon-year stress test are not captured and would refine both the cost and the resilience estimates. The demand is a representative calibrated archetype rather than metered island load, because public hourly demand for individual off-grid islands is scarce; substituting a metered profile would sharpen the demand-side detail without altering the structure of the comparison. The operational model is a deterministic linear program with perfect foresight, which yields a lower bound on operating cost; rolling-horizon or stochastic operation would test the robustness of the cost figures, although the resilience results derive from a separate, foresight-free outage simulation. The frequency analysis is a reduced-order primary-response screen (aggregated swing dynamics with first-order droop and grid-forming virtual inertia); it establishes feasibility and ranks contingencies but is not a substitute for electromagnetic-transient simulation, secondary/automatic generation control, under-frequency load-shedding scheme design, or protection-coordination studies, which are needed before deployment and which the marginal `battery_hydrogen` RoCoF and the diesel-trip nadir both motivate. The system is a single island with PV as the only renewable resource; wind hybridisation, inter-island interconnection, and component degradation over the project life are left to future work. Finally, hydrogen-technology costs are evolving rapidly; the capital-multiplier sweep partially internalises this, but the crossover should be re-evaluated as cost data mature.

## 5. Conclusion

For islanded power systems, the choice between a PV–diesel–battery and a PV–battery–hydrogen portfolio is governed by a quantifiable cost–resilience threshold rather than by a qualitative preference. Using an 8760-h cost-optimal dispatch model driven by real NASA POWER weather for a representative Philippine off-grid island, together with a worst-case outage simulation and a reduced-order frequency screen, we find that the battery–hydrogen portfolio is the defensible design under either of two independently realistic conditions: a delivered diesel cost at or above 377 USD MWh$^{-1}$ (342 USD MWh$^{-1}$ when avoided unserved energy is valued), or any material degradation of diesel support during outages—a derate below 50%, a fuel budget under 12 hours, or any start-up delay—each of which collapses the diesel portfolio's worst-case survivability to near zero in both energy and frequency-dynamic terms while the hydrogen store sustains full critical-load service for 48 hours. The threshold's position is set principally by PV capital cost, making PV-cost reduction the most effective lever on hydrogen competitiveness, while electrolyser and fuel-cell efficiency are nearly immaterial; and a matched-backbone test shows the resilience advantage to be intrinsic to the long-duration hydrogen store rather than a by-product of the larger backbone the store requires. These conditions correspond to exactly the remote-island and storm contingencies that motivate off-grid hydrogen, converting a qualitative intuition into design-actionable thresholds, attributions, and feasibility checks that island planners can apply directly.
