## 3. Results

### 3.1 Base portfolios and hydrogen-tank right-sizing

At the unit costs of Table 1 and a zero carbon price, the three portfolios have annual costs of 2.26, 3.97, and 21.3 M USD yr$^{-1}$ for `diesel_battery`, `battery_hydrogen`, and `battery_only`, with levelised costs of 217, 381, and 2511 USD MWh$^{-1}$ respectively (Table 2). The `battery_only` portfolio is dominated by unserved-energy penalty—PV and a diurnal battery alone cannot firm the load through extended low-irradiance periods—and is retained only as a renewable-only reference; the analysis that follows compares `diesel_battery` and `battery_hydrogen`. Because only the diesel portfolio emits, the carbon price shifts the `diesel_battery` cost (and the crossover) but leaves the other two unchanged.

A pre-analysis of the hydrogen design exposed a capital oversizing carried by the original fixed-capacity specification. The 50,000 kg hydrogen tank accounted for 47% of the `battery_hydrogen` capital while providing storage an order of magnitude beyond the design's actual need, which is bounded by two requirements that are both far below 50,000 kg. First, the cost-optimal annual dispatch never stores more than 5,669 kg: the hydrogen inventory follows a seasonal trajectory, drawn down to empty in mid-January and refilled to its 5,669 kg annual peak by December (Fig. 2), so any capacity above this is operationally idle. Second, critical-load resilience is invariant to tank capacity over the tested range 5,000–50,000 kg—the worst-case critical-load-served ratio (CLSR) remains 1.0 with full 48-h survivability across 50 outage seeds and three durations. The resilience requirement is modest because the binding worst-case outage is a low-irradiance, nighttime-onset event in which the fuel cell must supply the critical load through the dark hours, drawing on the order of 1,200 kg of hydrogen over 48 h—well within the 5,000 kg inventory held at 50% of a 10,000 kg tank; favourable daytime outages are instead covered directly by PV and battery, with surplus PV even replenishing the store. We therefore right-size the tank to 10,000 kg, which accommodates the 5,669 kg operational peak with margin and covers the worst-case outage draw several-fold over. This lowers the `battery_hydrogen` levelised cost from 610 to 381 USD MWh$^{-1}$ with no change in resilience and no change in the optimised operating cost—a pure elimination of capital embedded by the fixed-capacity design. All subsequent results use the 10,000 kg tank; Figure 2 shows a representative week of dispatch for both portfolios, with daytime PV charging the battery and electrolyser and the battery (and, in low-PV periods, the fuel cell) serving the evening peak.

### 3.2 Cost–resilience threshold

Figure 3 maps the annual-cost difference between the two portfolios across delivered diesel cost (100–1000 USD MWh$^{-1}$) and a hydrogen-CAPEX multiplier (0.30–1.20), at the central carbon price of 150 USD tCO$_2^{-1}$ and a 48-h outage. The zero-difference contour—the economic crossover—passes through **377 USD MWh$^{-1}$** delivered diesel at the baseline hydrogen CAPEX (multiplier 1.0): below this the `diesel_battery` portfolio is cheaper, above it `battery_hydrogen` is. A delivered diesel cost of 377 USD MWh$^{-1}$ corresponds to roughly 3.8 USD L$^{-1}$, within the range reported for remote Philippine islands where maritime fuel logistics dominate delivered cost. Reducing the hydrogen CAPEX to 0.30 of baseline lowers the crossover to 216 USD MWh$^{-1}$; raising it to 1.20 lifts it to 423 USD MWh$^{-1}$ (Table 3). Internalising the resilience value of avoided unserved energy at VOLL = 5000 USD MWh$^{-1}$ (Fig. 3b) lowers the crossover to **342 USD MWh$^{-1}$**, because the diesel portfolio's residual outage exposure is then priced. The crossover is interior to the swept diesel range for every multiplier level and reproduces, at the overlapping points, the coarse three-value sensitivity of the original manuscript—now resolved as a continuous frontier rather than a single break-even point.

### 3.3 What moves the frontier

Figure 4 decomposes the crossover diesel cost into its sensitivity to each design parameter, varied across its plausible range with the others held at baseline. The crossover is most sensitive by far to PV capital cost—a span of 148 USD MWh$^{-1}$ across 0.6–1.2× of baseline—followed by hydrogen-tank cost (92), electrolyser cost (66), and fuel-cell cost (45); battery-energy cost contributes 11, and the electrolyser and fuel-cell efficiencies move the crossover by less than 1 USD MWh$^{-1}$ (Table 5). The dominance of PV cost is structural, not incidental: the `battery_hydrogen` portfolio carries 14 MW of PV against the `diesel_battery` portfolio's 2.8 MW, so a change in PV cost bears roughly five-fold more strongly on the hydrogen side; normalised per unit fractional cost change, PV is about 2.5× more influential than the hydrogen tank. The near-zero efficiency sensitivities confirm that within this comparison the hydrogen subsystem is sized by energy balance and resilience, not by conversion efficiency. The actionable implication is that PV-cost reduction—rather than electrolyser or fuel-cell improvement—is the single most effective lever for hydrogen competitiveness in islanded systems, a conclusion that answers the request for the design levers that move the Pareto frontier.

### 3.4 Is the resilience advantage intrinsic? A matched-backbone test

Section 3.3 shows the frontier is dominated by PV cost, which raises the question of whether the hydrogen portfolio's apparent advantages merely reflect its larger PV–battery backbone. Figure 5 isolates the flexibility technology by placing both portfolios on a common backbone—varying PV (7/10/14 MW) and battery energy (8/12 MWh) identically (energy-to-power ratio fixed at 6 h) and retaining only the distinguishing diesel or hydrogen subsystem. Two results follow. First, the hydrogen portfolio requires a PV backbone of at least 14 MW to be viable at all: at 7–10 MW the electrolyser cannot be supplied enough surplus to balance the 33.5%-efficient hydrogen round-trip, the portfolio incurs large unserved-energy penalties, and no crossover exists within the swept diesel range (Table 4). The large PV of the hydrogen design is thus a physical requirement, not arbitrary oversizing. Second, and decisively, the resilience gap persists under a matched backbone: at an identical 14 MW / 12 MWh backbone the `diesel_battery` portfolio sustains only about one hour of the worst 48-h outage (worst-case CLSR 0.97 across ten seeds) while `battery_hydrogen` sustains the full 48 hours (CLSR 1.0), and the `diesel_battery` worst-case survivability never reaches the hydrogen level at any backbone tested. Because the only difference at a matched backbone is the flexibility subsystem, the resilience advantage is attributable to the long-duration hydrogen store itself—answering directly the concern that fixed capacities, rather than the technology, drive the result.

### 3.5 Robustness to the diesel-support assumption

The resilience comparison so far assumes diesel is unavailable during an outage. Figure 6 relaxes this across graded support cases. Under full unavailability (D0) the `diesel_battery` worst-case survivability is zero hours and the worst-case CLSR is 0.57 across ten seeds—the single-seed value of 0.64 in the original manuscript was optimistic. Restoring diesel support closes the gap only above clear thresholds: the `diesel_battery` portfolio matches the hydrogen portfolio's 48-h survivability only when diesel is available at 50% or more of rated power (D1) or with a fuel budget of 12 h or more (D2). Most strikingly, any start-up or delivery delay collapses worst-case survivability to zero—even a six-hour delay (D3)—because the battery depletes before diesel arrives during the worst-timed outage. When diesel is instead fully available but its delivered fuel cost is shocked (D4), the economic crossover is unchanged at 377 USD MWh$^{-1}$; above roughly 380 USD MWh$^{-1}$ the `battery_hydrogen` portfolio is simultaneously cheaper and at least as resilient. The hydrogen portfolio's resilience value is therefore precisely a hedge against degradation of diesel support—derating, fuel limits, or delivery delay—which are the characteristic failure modes of the storms and logistics disruptions that island contingencies entail.

### 3.6 Dynamic feasibility

Figure 7 screens primary-frequency response. Under a 10% load step at the evening-peak operating point all three portfolios hold their frequency nadir above 49.0 Hz; the rate of change of frequency is 0.86 Hz s$^{-1}$ for `diesel_battery` (within both islanded and mainland limits, owing to synchronous-generator inertia), 1.88 Hz s$^{-1}$ for `battery_only`, and 2.06 Hz s$^{-1}$ for `battery_hydrogen`—the last marginally above the 2.0 Hz s$^{-1}$ islanded screening value, which a modest increase of the grid-forming virtual-inertia constant to $\ge 1.2$ s removes (Table 6). The portfolios diverge sharply only in the contingency that matters most for an islanded system: loss of the diesel generator. When the 1.65 MW diesel trips and the 1.5 MW battery must assume grid-forming, the instantaneous RoCoF reaches 27 Hz s$^{-1}$ (5.9 Hz s$^{-1}$ averaged over the relay-relevant 500 ms window) and the nadir falls to 45.9 Hz, because the battery is undersized relative to the generation it must instantly replace; full rebalancing requires under-frequency load shedding, though the battery alone can sustain the 0.55 critical fraction (1.5 MW $\ge$ 0.91 MW). The `diesel_battery` portfolio is thus dynamically weakest during exactly the event—diesel loss—at which Sections 3.4–3.5 found it energetically weakest, whereas `battery_hydrogen`, with a larger grid-forming battery, fuel-cell droop support, and no diesel to lose, avoids the contingency. The dynamic screen reinforces rather than qualifies the cost–resilience conclusion, and answers the request to treat frequency stability and the grid-forming reference explicitly for an islanded system.

### 3.7 Synthesis

Taken together (Fig. 3–7), the battery–hydrogen portfolio is the defensible design under either of two independently realistic conditions: a delivered diesel cost at or above 377 USD MWh$^{-1}$ (§3.2, §3.5-D4), or any material degradation of diesel support during outages (§3.5-D0–D3), under which the diesel–battery worst-case survivability collapses—in both energy (§3.4–3.5) and frequency-dynamic (§3.6) terms—while the hydrogen store holds full critical-load service. Both conditions characterise remote-island and typhoon contingencies. The hydrogen design's large PV is a physical requirement of the round-trip (§3.4), and PV-cost reduction is the most powerful lever on its competitiveness (§3.3).
